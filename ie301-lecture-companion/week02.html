Logic (slide 17): f concave ⇒ f′(x) > 0 means x* > x (x becomes the lower bound); f′(x) < 0 means x* < x. Stop when the bracket is within 2ε (slide 19). It always converges — but slowly, and it ignores the curvature information in f″ (slide 22).

Newton’s method: ride the parabola Slides 23–28

Same problem. Newton fits a quadratic (2nd-order Taylor) at the current point and jumps to its peak: xi+1 = xi − f′(xi)/f″(xi). The dashed parabola shows the approximation; the hollow dot is where the next step lands. Try different starting points — compare the iteration count with bisection’s.

Fast local convergence, but not globally convergent (slide 28): with a small x₀ here, f″ ≈ 0 and the first jump flies far off the chart before crawling back. Newton can also land on a minimum or saddle — it only looks for f′ = 0.

Find every extremum candidate Slides 29–34

This f(x) on [0, 10] is not concave and not differentiable everywhere. Click every point that could be a local max or min: stationary points (f′ = 0), points where f′ doesn’t exist, and the endpoints. Then check your answer.

found: 0 / 4

The three candidate types (slide 33): stationary points a < x < b with f′(x) = 0; points where f′(x) does not exist (kinks); and the endpoints a, b. The global optimum is the best candidate — and here it hides at an endpoint, where the derivative never vanishes.

The monopolist’s sweet spot Slides 35–37

Producing x units costs $5/unit and each sells for (10 − x) dollars. Slide the production level and watch profit P(x) = x(10 − x) − 5x = 5x − x². Vote first: produce more or fewer than 3 units?
