Beat me if you can: the match puzzle Slides 4–7

30 matches. On each turn take 1, 2 or 3 — whoever takes the last match loses. You move first. The computer plays the DP strategy. Can anyone in class beat it? (There is exactly one way.)

matches left: 30

Your move. Think backwards: which counts would you hate to face?

spoiler — open after the class has answeredWorking backwards (slides 6–7): facing 1 match you lose; facing 2–4 you win; facing 5 you lose… the losing counts are 1, 5, 9, 13, 17, 21, 25, 29 — every 4k+1. From 30, taking 1 leaves 29 and you can then mirror every opponent move to keep leaving 4k+1. That backward sweep IS dynamic programming.

Why DP wins: count the additions Slides 19–22

A layered network: 1 source, m middle stages with k cities each, 1 destination. Scale it up and compare brute-force enumeration against DP.

5
5
paths: — enumeration additions: — DP additions: — DP is —× cheaper

Slide 22’s numbers appear at m = 5, k = 5: 3125 paths → 15 625 additions by enumeration, but only (m − 1)k² + k = 105 by DP. Enumeration grows exponentially (kᵐ); DP grows linearly in the number of stages. That gap is the entire case for the method.
