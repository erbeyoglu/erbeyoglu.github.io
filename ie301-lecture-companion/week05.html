Spot Q and c Slides 4–7

Write the objective as f(x) = cᵀx − ½ xᵀQx (with Q symmetric). Fill in the entries and check — the −½ and the cross-term split are where everyone slips.

Slide 7’s recipe: qⱼⱼ = −2 × (coefficient of xⱼ²) and qᵢⱼ = qⱼᵢ = −(coefficient of xᵢxⱼ), because the ½ halves the diagonal and the symmetric pair qᵢⱼ + qⱼᵢ shares the cross term. Q positive semidefinite ⇔ f concave ⇔ KKT is necessary AND sufficient (slide 9).

The modified simplex machine Slides 14–38

The slides’ example: max 5x₁ − x₁² + 7x₂ − x₂² s.t. 4x₁ + x₂ ≤ 20, x₁ + 4x₂ ≤ 20, x ≥ 0, rewritten through its KKT system with artificials z₁, z₂ (slide 31). Click a column header to choose the entering variable — but respect the restricted-entry rule: a variable whose complementary partner is basic is off-limits. The map on the right shows (x₁, x₂) after every pivot.

Complementary pairs: (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), (u₁, v₁), (u₂, v₂). The restricted-entry rule (slide 21) keeps xᵀy + uᵀv = 0 at every step, so plain phase-1 simplex quietly solves a nonlinear program. When both artificials hit zero, the tableau’s x is the QPP optimum: (2.5, 3.5), Z = 18.5 (slide 38) — an interior optimum, so u₁ = u₂ = 0.
