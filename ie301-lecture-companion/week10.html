Bayes’ rule (slides 10–12): P(sick | +) = P(+|sick)P(sick) / [P(+|sick)P(sick) + P(+|healthy)P(healthy)]. At the slide’s numbers it is only 9.02% — the 1% false-positive rate applied to 9 990 healthy people swamps the handful of true positives. Rare condition ⇒ the prior dominates.

Which state does the liberal voter live in? Slide 13

State A: 40% of the population, 50% support. State B: 25%, 60%. State C: 35%, 35%. A random voter supports the liberal candidate — vote first: A, B or C? Then reveal how the areas decide it.

Your vote:

Bar width = population share P(Sᵢ); the filled part = supporters P(support | Sᵢ)·P(Sᵢ). Bayes just asks: what fraction of the total filled area is in each state? The answer is A, not B — B has the highest support rate, but A’s bigger population wins: P(A|support) = 0.423, P(B|support) = 0.317.

The 10-year-old radio Slides 27–29, 35

Memorylessness (slide 28): P(X > t + h | X > t) = P(X > h) — the survival curve beyond t, rescaled, is an exact copy of the original. For the radio, P = e⁻¹ ≈ 0.368 at ANY age. A uniform lifetime has memory: the same 10-year question decays to zero. This one property powers everything in the Markov weeks ahead.

N(t) ~ Poisson(λt) ⟺ gaps ~ exponential(λ) (slide 46). The highway answer is P(N = 0 in 10 s) = e^(−3·10/60) = e^(−0.5) ≈ 0.61 — and because increments are independent, it does not matter when you start crossing. The time of the n-th arrival is Erlang (slide 47).
