Convexity lab Slides 24–33

Class vote first: convex or not? Then test it — click two points inside the set; the line segment between them is drawn. If any part of the segment escapes, the set is not convex (slide 25).

Pick two points inside the set.

Definition (slide 25): S is convex if λx + (1−λ)y ∈ S for all x, y ∈ S and λ ∈ [0,1]. One escaping segment is a proof of non-convexity; staying inside once is not a proof of convexity.

Vote: convex, concave, both, or neither? Drag the two handles x′ and x″ along the curve and slide λ — watch whether the chord stays above or below the function (slides 27–28).

λ = 0.55
Your vote:

Convex: f(λx′ + (1−λ)x″) ≤ λf(x′) + (1−λ)f(x″) — the chord is never below the curve. One-variable test (slide 33): f″ ≥ 0 everywhere ⇔ convex; f″ ≤ 0 everywhere ⇔ concave.

Convex or concave? — the Hessian test Slides 34–39

Build a quadratic f(x₁, x₂) = a·x₁² + b·x₁x₂ + c·x₂². Look at the surface and the Hessian, apply the principal-minor theorem (slide 37) in your head, and vote — then check.

Theorem (slide 37): f is convex on S ⇔ all principal minors of H are ≥ 0. f is concave ⇔ all nonzero principal minors of order k have the sign of (−1)ᵏ. For this 2×2 Hessian: first principal minors are the diagonal entries 2a and 2c; the second principal minor is det H = 4ac − b².
